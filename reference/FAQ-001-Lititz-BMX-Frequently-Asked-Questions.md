## About Lititz BMX

### 1. What is Lititz BMX?

Lititz BMX is an independent BMX archive, preservation project, media platform, and developing private museum and vintage-rental experience based in Lititz, Pennsylvania.

The project preserves BMX history through physical artifacts, oral histories, rider and manufacturer profiles, educational resources, historical research, original media, and a continually expanding digital archive.

The goal is not simply to collect or display old bikes. Lititz BMX connects artifacts to the people, places, decisions, and stories that give them meaning, preserving those connections for riders, families, researchers, and future generations.

### 2. How did the idea for Lititz BMX begin?

Lititz BMX did not begin as a traditional business plan. It grew from a private collection and the realization that many important BMX artifacts were becoming separated from the stories that made them historically significant.

One artifact led to another. One story led to an interview. One relationship opened the door to another part of BMX history.

As the collection expanded, so did the responsibility to document provenance, preserve oral histories, organize historical information, and make the material useful to others. What began as a personal effort to preserve meaningful pieces of BMX history gradually developed into a structured digital archive, original media platform, educational resource, and long-term museum and vintage-rental concept. The project continues to grow from the same basic belief: BMX history deserves to be preserved properly, and it should be experienced rather than merely stored.

### 3. What role did Harry Leary play in the creation of Lititz BMX?

Harry Leary was the catalyst and the first major historical through-line of Lititz BMX.

The effort to preserve artifacts, memories, interviews, photographs, family history, and personal stories connected to Harry demonstrated how quickly important parts of BMX history can become scattered, disconnected, or lost.

Harry’s career touched professional racing, factory teams, product development, DIRTWERX, mentorship, media, and multiple generations of riders. Preserving his story naturally opened the door to documenting the people, companies, events, and culture surrounding him.

Lititz BMX is not limited to Harry Leary, but his legacy provided the foundation from which the larger preservation project grew. Without Harry, his family, the artifacts connected to his life, and the BMX community surrounding his story, Lititz BMX would not exist in its present form.

### 4. Who founded Lititz BMX?

Lititz BMX was founded by Kyle A. Huffman, a retired instructional designer, twice-deployed U.S. Navy veteran, lifelong BMX enthusiast, collector, researcher, interviewer, and media producer based in Lititz, Pennsylvania.

Kyle continues to independently develop and manage the project, including artifact acquisition and cataloging, historical research, interviews, educational resources, digital archive development, media production, preservation standards, partnerships, and the long-term museum concept.

What began as a personal effort to preserve meaningful artifacts has grown into a structured archive and public educational resource with an increasingly broad view of BMX history and culture.

### 5. What experience does Kyle A. Huffman bring to the project?

Kyle does not come from a conventional museum career. He brings a cross-disciplinary background built around research, training, systems, technology, storytelling, project development, and organizational leadership.

He holds an MBA and a Master of Science in Education and earned the Associate Professional in Talent Development credential. His professional background includes U.S. Navy intelligence and technical training, defense consulting, global technical-support leadership, curriculum development, instructional design, media production, learning technology, customer experience, and organizational development.

Over more than two decades, Kyle developed training systems, educational content, technical resources, and digital learning experiences for organizations including the U.S. Navy, Booz Allen Hamilton, Barracuda Networks, The Hershey Company, and Fulton Bank. He also founded and served as the inaugural chairperson of Fulton Bank’s Veterans Employee Resource Group.

Those experiences now influence how Lititz BMX:

- Catalogs and organizes historical information
- Develops learning resources and digital exhibits
- Conducts and preserves interviews
- Creates videos, podcasts, and enhanced media
- Establishes consistent documentation standards
- Manages large, interconnected collections of content
- Transforms individual artifacts into accessible historical stories

The result is an approach that combines the passion of a lifelong BMX enthusiast with the structure of an instructional designer and the long-term planning of a project manager.

### 6. Is Lititz BMX a museum?

Lititz BMX is best described as a developing private museum/education center supported by an active public digital archive.

It is not currently a conventional walk-in museum with regular daily operating hours. The physical collection is being developed as an intimate, story-driven museum, archive, and media space where artifacts can be experienced within their historical context.

At the same time, the online Lititz BMX Archive already serves as the public-facing portion of the museum. It allows people from around the world to explore artifacts, collections, interviews, rider profiles, educational resources, campaigns, and original historical research.

The physical and digital experiences are intended to work together. The museum preserves the objects, while the archive preserves and shares the stories connected to them.

### 7. Will Lititz BMX be open to the public?

The long-term plan is to welcome visitors through small, appointment-based experiences rather than operate as a high-volume, walk-in museum.

This approach fits the physical setting and allows each visit to remain personal, guided, and responsive to the interests of the visitor or group. It also helps protect rare and historically significant artifacts while maintaining the intimate character of the collection.

Public access will expand responsibly as space, zoning, insurance, preservation requirements, and operating procedures allow.

In the meantime, the digital archive remains open to everyone. Future public opportunities may also include private tours, special programs, educational visits, collaborative exhibits, pop-up displays, media events, and selected vintage BMX experiences.

### 8. What can visitors expect to experience?

Visitors should expect more than rows of old bicycles. The goal is a guided, museum-style experience combining complete bikes, prototypes, race-worn jerseys, helmets, awards, artwork, photographs, documents, memorabilia, oral histories, videos, and other historically significant artifacts.

Each object is presented as part of a larger story. Digital archive pages and QR-linked resources can allow visitors to explore photographs, interviews, documentation, provenance, related artifacts, and historical context while viewing the physical object.

Because visits are expected to remain small, the experience can be shaped around the interests of each group, including:

- BMX racing and freestyle history
- Specific riders, teams, or manufacturers
- Bicycle design and restoration
- Harry Leary and DIRTWERX
- BMX in film, music, and popular culture
- Local and international BMX history
- Collecting, provenance, and preservation
- Media production and oral-history work

As the vintage-rental portion of the project develops, selected experiences may eventually allow visitors to ride and feel a piece of BMX history rather than only view it from behind a display.

### 9. Is Lititz BMX affiliated with Rock Lititz?

No. Lititz BMX is an independent project and is not currently affiliated with Rock Lititz.

The connection is geographic and strategic. Lititz BMX is located only a few minutes from the Rock Lititz campus and exists within the same broader community of music, live production, craftsmanship, tourism, independent business, and creative work.

That proximity may create future opportunities involving visitors, artists, touring professionals, media projects, cultural tourism, or collaborations. However, Lititz BMX should not be represented as a Rock Lititz company, attraction, or official partner unless a formal relationship is established in the future.

### 10. Why is Lititz BMX located in Lititz, Pennsylvania?

Lititz is home, but it is also an unusually strong environment in which to develop a project like Lititz BMX. The town has a well-established identity built around history, craftsmanship, independent businesses, tourism, music, creativity, and community. It is part of Lancaster County’s larger visitor economy, is located near the Rock Lititz campus, and is accessible to communities throughout Pennsylvania and the Mid-Atlantic region.

Lititz also has its own connection to BMX history that deserves to be documented and preserved. Building the project here allows Lititz BMX to remain both local and outward-facing: rooted in a specific community while preserving a sport, culture, and collection with national and international reach. The name reflects more than a physical address. It represents the place from which the archive is being built and the community in which its future museum experience is intended to grow.

## Archive & Collection

### 11. What kinds of artifacts are preserved?

Lititz BMX preserves a broad range of objects connected to BMX racing, freestyle, manufacturing, media, and culture.

The physical collection includes complete bicycles, frames, prototypes, race-worn jerseys, helmets,

trophies, awards, number plates, bicycle components, team and manufacturer memorabilia, original artwork, photographs, correspondence, documents, tools, promotional materials, and personal items connected to riders and significant moments in BMX history.

The archive is not limited to rare or expensive objects. An ordinary item can become historically important when its ownership, use, or story helps document a rider, event, company, community, or period in the sport.

Lititz BMX also preserves oral histories, interviews, videos, digital photographs, research, and other historical records. These materials may be maintained as archive records even when they are not assigned a physical artifact number.

The goal is not simply to accumulate objects. It is to preserve meaningful BMX history and the stories that give those objects value.

### 12. How many artifacts are currently in the archive?

At the time of publication, Lititz BMX has formally cataloged more than 100 physical artifacts, with at least 200 additional items awaiting full cataloging, research, photography, or documentation.

Because Lititz BMX is an active and continually growing archive, the exact number changes regularly as new items are acquired, existing materials are separated into individual records, and previously undocumented objects are formally added to the catalog.

The physical artifact count also represents only one portion of the project. The larger digital archive includes hundreds of pages documenting riders, teams, manufacturers, tracks, interviews, campaigns, educational resources, media, and other areas of BMX history.

### 13. How are artifacts cataloged?

Each physical artifact is assigned one permanent Lititz BMX artifact number when it is formally entered into the archive.

The identifier follows the Lititz BMX numbering standard and connects the physical object to its inventory record, archive page, photographs, videos, QR codes, research notes, educational materials, and related records.

Depending on the artifact and the available information, its record may include:

- The artifact’s title and physical description
- Known ownership and use history
- Associated riders, teams, manufacturers, races, or events
- Signatures, serial numbers, markings, and identifying features
- Photographs and supporting documents
- Interview or oral-history references
- Condition and restoration information
- The source of the artifact
- Connections to related artifacts and archive materials

Cataloging is treated as an ongoing research process. A record can become more detailed as additional

documentation, photographs, interviews, or historical information are discovered, but the artifact’s permanent identifier remains unchanged.

### 14. Why are artifact numbers permanent?

Permanent numbers protect the historical continuity of the archive.

An artifact may be moved to a different display, placed into storage, reorganized on the website, connected to a newly created collection, or better understood as new research becomes available. Its identifier should not change every time the archive changes around it.

A permanent number allows photographs, videos, QR codes, research papers, educational materials, inventory records, and future publications to continue referring to the same physical object.

Artifact numbers are intentionally non-descriptive. They do not attempt to encode the rider, manufacturer, category, date, or historical importance of the object because those interpretations may evolve.

If an artifact permanently leaves the collection, its number is retired and is never reassigned to another object. The historical record remains intact even when the artifact is no longer physically held by Lititz BMX.

### 15. Can artifacts be donated or loaned?

Lititz BMX is open to discussing donations, loans, and other forms of contribution on a case-by-case basis.

Acceptance depends on factors such as historical relevance, provenance, physical condition, available storage, preservation requirements, duplication within the collection, legal ownership, and the archive’s ability to responsibly document and care for the material.

Not every contribution requires transferring ownership. In some cases, the most valuable contribution may be permission to photograph an artifact, scan documents, record an interview, preserve a story, or create a digital archive record while the original remains with its owner or family.

Any physical loan should be documented in writing and clearly identify ownership, the duration and purpose of the loan, care requirements, photography and publication permissions, transportation responsibilities, and return arrangements.

The goal is not to acquire everything offered to Lititz BMX. The goal is to preserve the right material responsibly and in a way that respects the artifact, its owner, and the history connected to it.

### 16. How is provenance documented?

Provenance is the documented history of an artifact: who owned it, where it came from, how it was used, and why it is historically meaningful.

Lititz BMX documents provenance using the strongest available combination of evidence, which may include:

- Ownership history
- Information supplied by riders, families, teams, manufacturers, or previous owners
- Photographs showing the artifact in use
- Serial numbers, signatures, labels, and identifying marks
- Receipts, letters, certificates, shipping records, and correspondence
- Race results, publications, advertisements, catalogs, and media coverage
- Interviews and recorded oral histories
- Connections to related artifacts, clothing, awards, or photographs

Whenever possible, confirmed information is distinguished from reasonable attribution, personal recollection, or information that remains uncertain.

Not every artifact arrives with a complete history. Part of the archive’s work is to preserve what is known, identify what is not yet known, and continue researching without presenting assumptions as established fact.

### 17. Are replica bikes identified as replicas?

Yes. But there are currently no replica bikes in the Lititz BMX inventory. Replica, reconstruction, tribute, and period-correct builds are clearly identified and are not represented as original historical artifacts.

The archive record should explain what the bike is, why it was created, what source material was used, and which parts, if any, are original, period-correct, reproduced, or modern replacements.

Lititz BMX recognizes that the original object is not always available. It may have been destroyed, altered, separated from its history, placed into a private collection, or lost from public view.

In those situations, a carefully researched and honestly presented replica can preserve important information about the original bike’s appearance, geometry, components, historical context, and final known story.

The value of a replica is not based on pretending that it is the original. Its value comes from transparency, research, education, and its ability to preserve a chapter of BMX history that might otherwise disappear.

### 18. What happens if new historical information is discovered?

The archive record is updated.

Lititz BMX treats historical research as an ongoing process rather than a finished product. New photographs, interviews, documents, ownership records, or firsthand accounts may clarify an artifact’s history, correct an earlier description, or reveal a connection that was not previously known.

When credible new information is discovered, the description, provenance, attribution, related links, and supporting documentation may be revised or expanded.

The permanent artifact number does not change.

Corrections should be made transparently rather than ignored or concealed. When information remains disputed or cannot be fully verified, the record should explain the uncertainty and distinguish documented

facts from competing accounts or informed interpretation.

Preserving history includes being willing to improve the record when better evidence becomes available.

## Digital Archive

### 19. Can I browse the archive online?

Yes.

The Lititz BMX Archive is available online through LititzBMX.com, which directs visitors to the complete digital archive.

The archive contains hundreds of interconnected pages documenting physical artifacts, collections, riders, teams, manufacturers, tracks, campaigns, interviews, educational resources, BMX in popular culture, and original Lititz BMX media.

Many physical artifacts have individual archive pages containing photographs, descriptions, provenance information, related videos, and links to connected people or historical topics. Other items remain in various stages of cataloging, research, photography, and page development.

The online archive is an active part of the project rather than simply a promotional website. It serves as the public-facing digital museum, research resource, and long-term record of the collection.

### 20. Why is the archive free to explore?

Lititz BMX believes access to BMX history should not depend on a person’s location, ability to travel, or ability to pay an admission fee.

Making the digital archive freely available allows riders, families, researchers, collectors, students, and longtime BMX enthusiasts to explore the collection from anywhere in the world.

Free access also supports the preservation process. Publicly available records can help reconnect artifacts with former owners, identify people in photographs, correct incomplete information, preserve firsthand memories, and lead to new historical discoveries.

Lititz BMX remains independently developed and maintained, and future museum visits, rentals, merchandise, special programs, or partnerships may help support its continued growth. However, the core digital archive is intended to remain broadly accessible as an educational and preservation resource. The archive has greater historical value when people can use it, learn from it, and contribute to the stories it preserves.

### 21. How often is new content added?

New content is added regularly as artifacts are cataloged, interviews are recorded, research is completed, photographs are processed, and new areas of BMX history are developed.

There is no fixed publishing schedule. Some updates may involve a single correction or newly discovered detail, while others may include complete artifact pages, rider profiles, educational resources, interview collections, campaign materials, or major additions to the digital archive.

Existing pages may also be expanded when new photographs, documents, oral histories, provenance information, or related artifacts become available.

Because Lititz BMX is an active preservation project, the archive is never considered completely finished. It is designed to grow and improve over time while maintaining permanent references to previously cataloged artifacts and published materials.

### 22. What educational resources are available?

Lititz BMX develops educational materials that help visitors explore BMX history through more than artifact descriptions alone.

Resources may include:

- Rider, team, manufacturer, and track profiles
- Artifact-based historical lessons
- BMX terminology and reference materials
- Interactive word searches and quizzes
- Podcast and interview scavenger hunts
- Book-club resources
- Timelines and historical research
- International BMX history
- BMX in film, music, television, and popular culture
- Campaign-based learning activities
- Oral histories and long-form interviews
- Enhanced media combining interviews with supporting photographs, documents, and artifacts

These resources are designed for different levels of familiarity with BMX. A visitor may arrive as a researcher, former racer, collector, parent, student, or someone encountering the sport’s history for the first time.

The educational mission is influenced by founder Kyle A. Huffman’s background in instructional design, curriculum development, technical training, and digital learning. The goal is to make BMX history organized, engaging, understandable, and useful without stripping away the personality and lived experiences that make the sport’s history meaningful.

### 23. What are Featured Archive Highlights?

Featured Archive Highlights are rotating selections of artifacts, collections, campaigns, rider profiles, educational resources, and historical topics from within the Lititz BMX Archive.

They are used in recording descriptions, media pages, and other Lititz BMX content to help visitors move

beyond the page or video where they first encountered the project.

A person watching an interview may be directed to a related jersey, rider profile, campaign, international history page, or educational resource. Another recording may feature a completely different group of archive entries.

The highlights are intentionally rotated so that the same small group of well-known artifacts does not receive all of the attention. This gives lesser-known objects and stories additional opportunities to be discovered while demonstrating the breadth of the archive.

Featured Archive Highlights turn each recording or public-facing page into another entrance to the larger Lititz BMX preservation project.

### 24. Why are there so many internal links?

BMX history is interconnected.

A single artifact may relate to a rider, team, manufacturer, race, photograph, interview, campaign, period in the sport, or another object in the collection. Internal links allow visitors to follow those relationships rather than viewing each page as an isolated record.

For example, a race-worn jersey may link to the rider who wore it, the team represented on it, a related interview, another artifact from the same period, and a broader collection page. Those connections provide context that a single description cannot fully communicate.

Internal links also help visitors navigate a large and continually expanding archive, discover material they may not have known to search for, and understand how individual stories fit within the larger history of BMX.

From a preservation standpoint, the links help create a structured network of related records. From a visitor standpoint, they make the archive feel less like a list of objects and more like an explorable museum.

The goal is not to keep visitors on one page. It is to help them follow the history wherever the story leads.

## Videos & Media

### 25. What types of videos does Lititz BMX produce?

Lititz BMX produces original media designed to document BMX history, preserve firsthand accounts, and connect viewers with the larger archive. Productions may include:

- Long-form rider, family, industry, and collector interviews
- Oral-history recordings
- Fireside BMX Chat conversations
- Pump Track Chat episodes
- Artifact unboxings and archive-entry sessions
- Collection tours and individual artifact features
- Restoration and preservation projects
- Campaign videos
- Event and community documentation
- Educational videos
- Short-form excerpts from longer recordings
- Behind-the-scenes archive and production content
- Enhanced Editions supported by photographs, documents, video, and related artifacts

Some recordings focus on nationally recognized riders or historically significant objects. Others preserve local stories, personal memories, and overlooked parts of BMX culture that may not have been formally documented elsewhere.

The purpose is not simply to create entertainment or promote the collection. Each recording is treated as part of the historical record and, whenever practical, is connected to related artifacts, profiles, collections, and educational resources within the Lititz BMX Archive.

### 26. What is an Enhanced Edition?

An Enhanced Edition is a visually enriched version of an interview or other recording.

Photographs, documents, historical footage, advertisements, race results, artifacts, and other supporting materials are synchronized with the conversation so viewers can see the people, objects, and events being discussed in real time.

The original speaker remains the center of the presentation. Supporting media is added to provide context, clarify references, and help preserve the relationship between the oral history and the surviving historical evidence.

An Enhanced Edition is not intended to rewrite or overproduce the original conversation. Its purpose is to make the recording more useful, engaging, and historically complete while preserving the speaker’s original meaning and personality.

Where appropriate, both the original recording and the Enhanced Edition may be preserved. The original protects the integrity of the source interview, while the enhanced version creates a more accessible documentary and educational experience.

### 27. Why are interviews preserved in long form?

Long-form interviews preserve context, personality, uncertainty, humor, emotion, and the natural connections between memories.

A short excerpt may capture one interesting statement, but it can also separate that statement from the larger conversation that explains what the speaker meant, how one event led to another, or why a particular memory mattered.

Preserving the complete interview provides a fuller historical record. It allows future viewers, families,

researchers, and filmmakers to hear the speaker’s story in their own voice rather than relying only on selected quotations or edited summaries.

Long-form preservation also recognizes that the importance of a conversation may change over time. A passing reference that seems minor today may later help identify an artifact, establish a timeline, confirm a relationship, or explain an overlooked chapter of BMX history.

Shorter clips and Enhanced Editions may make interviews easier to discover and explore, but the complete recording remains the primary oral-history source whenever practical.

### 28. Are Shorts connected to the archive?

Yes.

Lititz BMX Shorts are designed as entry points into the larger preservation project rather than as isolated social-media posts.

A Short may highlight a rider’s memory, an artifact, a historical event, a surprising detail, or a brief moment from a longer interview. Its description can then direct viewers to the complete recording, the Lititz BMX website, the digital archive, and rotating Featured Archive Highlights.

This allows someone who discovers Lititz BMX through a short video to continue exploring related riders, collections, artifacts, campaigns, and educational resources.

The Shorts also help preserve and organize important moments from longer recordings in a format that is easier to search, share, and discover. They do not replace the complete interviews. Instead, they act as individual doorways into the full story.

## Preservation Philosophy

### 30. Why preserve BMX history?

BMX history was built through more than championship results and famous bicycles. It was shaped by riders, families, local tracks, small manufacturers, race teams, photographers, artists, promoters, mechanics, volunteers, and communities that often documented their own history informally—or not at all. As time passes, bicycles are rebuilt, photographs become separated from their names, trophies are discarded, companies disappear, and firsthand memories are lost. Without deliberate preservation, important parts of BMX history can vanish even while the people who lived them are still here.

Lititz BMX preserves BMX history so that future riders, families, researchers, collectors, and enthusiasts can understand not only what survived, but what it meant.

The mission is to protect the relationships between objects, people, places, and stories. A bicycle can show how the sport developed. An interview can explain why it mattered. A jersey, photograph, letter, or small personal item can preserve the human side of the history.

BMX gave generations of riders a sense of identity, community, creativity, competition, and possibility. That cultural history deserves to be documented with the same seriousness given to other sports, industries, and forms of popular culture.

### 31. Why preserve ordinary BMX items instead of only famous bikes?

History is rarely contained in only the most valuable or recognizable objects.

A famous race bike may document an important championship or rider, but an ordinary number plate, team jersey, local trophy, handwritten letter, race flyer, shop decal, tool, or family photograph may preserve a story that exists nowhere else.

Objects that appear ordinary today can become historically significant when they help establish:

- Who participated in the sport
- How riders and teams were connected
- What local BMX communities looked like
- How bicycles and equipment were used
- How companies promoted themselves
- How racing, freestyle, fashion, and culture changed over time
- What BMX meant to the people who lived it

Lititz BMX does not determine importance solely by monetary value, rarity, or celebrity ownership. Context matters.

The archive preserves major artifacts when possible, but it also recognizes that the fuller history of BMX is found in the everyday material left behind by riders, families, tracks, shops, teams, and communities.

Sometimes the smallest object carries the only surviving evidence of a much larger story.

### 32. What makes an artifact historically significant?

Historical significance is determined by the relationship between an object and the story it can help document.

An artifact may be significant because of its connection to:

- A rider, team, manufacturer, track, event, or historical period
- A championship, race, product development effort, or important moment
- A prototype, unusual design, production change, or technological development
- A documented personal or community story
- A part of BMX history that is poorly represented elsewhere
- A surviving group of related objects, photographs, interviews, or records
- A clear chain of ownership or use

Rarity and monetary value can contribute to significance, but they are not the only factors.

A mass-produced object with strong provenance may be more historically useful than a rare object with no documented history. Likewise, an item connected to a local rider or small BMX community may preserve information that would otherwise disappear from the broader historical record.

Lititz BMX evaluates artifacts through a combination of provenance, documentation, physical characteristics, historical context, condition, research value, and their relationship to the rest of the archive.

Historical significance is not always fully understood when an artifact first arrives. Its importance may become clearer as additional evidence, interviews, or related materials are discovered.

### 33. Why document the stories behind artifacts?

An object without its story can quickly become little more than an old bicycle part, jersey, trophy, or photograph.

The story explains who owned it, how it was used, what happened around it, why it survived, and why someone believed it was worth preserving.

Documenting those stories helps establish provenance, but it also protects the human history that cannot be learned from the object alone. A race-worn jersey may reveal little about the rider’s experience until it is connected to an interview, photograph, result, team history, or family memory.

Stories can also explain imperfections that might otherwise be misunderstood. Scratches, repairs, modifications, handwriting, fading, or damage may be evidence of how an artifact was used rather than flaws that should be erased.

Lititz BMX uses artifact records, photographs, interviews, videos, supporting documents, oral histories, and related archive links to preserve as much context as reasonably possible.

The object is the evidence. The story is what gives that evidence meaning.

### 34. Why create replicas when originals no longer exist?

The original artifact will always be historically distinct from a replica. Lititz BMX does not treat replicas as substitutes for original objects or represent them as authentic originals.

However, an original bicycle or artifact may be destroyed, permanently altered, lost, inaccessible, or removed from public view. In those cases, a carefully researched and transparently documented replica can preserve information that might otherwise disappear.

A replica may help document:

- The original bicycle’s appearance and geometry
- Its period-correct components and setup
- Product-development or design history
- Connections between surviving photographs, documents, and oral histories
- The final known configuration or chapter of the original artifact
- How the object would have been experienced in physical space

The purpose is not to manufacture artificial provenance. The purpose is to create an educational interpretation based on the strongest available evidence.

Every replica, reconstruction, or tribute build should be clearly identified. Its record should explain what is original, reproduced, period-correct, modern, inferred, or still uncertain.

When the original can no longer tell its own story, a responsibly built replica can preserve the memory, form, educational value, and documented history surrounding it.

### 35. What is the long-term vision for Lititz BMX?

The long-term vision is to build Lititz BMX into a respected, independent center for BMX preservation, education, media, and experience.

The digital archive will continue expanding as a free public resource containing artifact records, collections, oral histories, rider and manufacturer profiles, educational materials, historical research, registries, timelines, original media, and interconnected reference resources.

The physical collection is being developed into an intimate, appointment-based museum experience in Lititz, Pennsylvania. Rather than operate as a large traditional museum, the goal is to create personal, guided visits where small groups can explore the artifacts, stories, and media surrounding BMX history. Future elements may include:

- A private boutique museum and archive
- Guided educational visits
- Selected vintage BMX rental or riding experiences
- Artifact-based exhibits and digital tours
- Oral-history and media-production facilities
- Enhanced interview and documentary projects
- Collaborative and traveling exhibits
- Research tools, registries, standards, and open reference materials
- Programs connecting BMX history with art, music, film, design, and local culture
- Partnerships with riders, families, manufacturers, collectors, educators, and community organizations

Lititz BMX is intended to remain independent, personal, and story-driven as it grows.

The ultimate goal is not to build the largest collection for the sake of size. It is to create a sustainable preservation project where physical artifacts, firsthand memories, educational resources, and original media work together to protect BMX history and make it meaningful to the next generation.

### 36. Why does Lititz BMX use preservation standards (LBS documents)?

Lititz BMX uses written preservation standards to make sure the archive remains consistent, understandable, and dependable as it grows.

A small collection can often be managed through memory and informal habits. A collection containing hundreds of artifacts, recordings, photographs, archive pages, educational resources, and interconnected historical records requires a more durable system.

Lititz BMX Standards—identified as LBS documents—establish repeatable practices for areas such as artifact numbering, recording descriptions, documentation, preservation, attribution, and public presentation.

These standards help Lititz BMX:

- Apply the same basic rules across artifacts and media
- Maintain permanent references as the archive changes
- Reduce errors, duplication, and inconsistent documentation
- Preserve institutional knowledge instead of relying only on one person’s memory
- Make future corrections and updates easier to track
- Explain publicly how the archive approaches its work
- Support future volunteers, collaborators, researchers, or successors
- Build a credible foundation for museum, educational, media, and partnership opportunities

The standards are not intended to make Lititz BMX feel overly institutional or bureaucratic. They provide enough structure to protect the history while allowing the archive to remain personal, creative, and responsive to new discoveries.

Each LBS document is treated as a living standard. It may be revised as the project develops, but changes should be documented, versioned, and made without unnecessarily disrupting permanent artifact numbers, published references, or existing historical records.

Using preservation standards reflects a simple principle: BMX history deserves more than good intentions. It deserves systems designed to help the information, objects, and stories remain useful long after they are first documented.
