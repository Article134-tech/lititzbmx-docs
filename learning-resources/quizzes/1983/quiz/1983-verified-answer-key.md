## Detailed verification

### 1. Judge

The controversy section states that BMXA:

> ran a two page spread on how-to-do the radical click-out, calling it a “Judge.”

The same section explains that Harry Leary later produced the earlier photographic proof and was awarded the signature jump.

**Answer:** Judge.

### 2. Richie Anderson

The Terrible Ten section states:

> Richie Anderson proceeded to DESTROY the amateur classes

**Answer:** C. Richie Anderson.

### 3. Gary Ellis

The article states:

> Gary Ellis waited ’til the last race of ’83 (the World Champs) to turn pro.

**Answer:** C. Gary Ellis.

### 4. False

The article says:

> Greg Hill won his very first NORA Cup at the beginning of the year. He was still dominating. Untouchable and riding for GT

It later says that around October he started GHP and that Gregory Jonathan Hill was born on December 28, 1983.

The compound statement is false because the team named in the quiz is wrong.

**Answer:** False.

### 5. $15,000

The ESPN Pro Series section states:

> $15,000 up for grabs at the seventh and final sucker.

**Answer:** $15,000.

### Extra credit — Brian Patterson

The final standings record states:

> Could’ve been higher, but broke his wrist at the finals. He still raced with it broken

**Answer:** Brian Patterson.

### Extra extra credit — Original

The source heading reads:

> THE ORIGINAL TERRIBLE TEN

The article introduction also says the May 1983 issue premiered the Terrible Ten.

**Answer:** Original.

## Critical verification findings

1. All seven published prompts have direct source support.
2. Question 4 is a deliberate compound-statement test: two portions are supported, but “riding for Hutch” is contradicted by the source.
3. The article attributes the trick-name controversy to several regional names—Judge, Leary, Medrano, and possibly Utterback—before awarding the signature jump to Harry Leary.
4. The $15,000 amount applies to the seventh and final ESPN-series race, while the first six races offered $5,000 each.
5. The package preserves the magazine’s colorful wording separately from the normalized answer summary.
